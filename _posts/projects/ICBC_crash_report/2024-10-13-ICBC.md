## Project Overview
This project research into the analysis of vehicular crashes in the Metro Vancouver area between 2018 and 2022. Using advanced data visualization tools, such as Tableau, we transformed raw data into actionable insights to help inform and improve road safety strategies. By examining various aspects of the data, including temporal patterns, geospatial distribution, crash types, and the correlation between crashes and population sizes, this project seeks to identify high-risk areas and propose targeted interventions.

## Project Objectives
The primary objectives of this project are to:
1. **Analyze the evolution of crashes** over the time period (2018-2022).
2. **Identify municipalities** with the highest number of crashes.
3. **Investigate correlations** between the frequency of crashes and population sizes.
4. **Compare crash rates per capita** between different municipalities.
5. **Examine the most frequent types of crashes** and the severity of the crashes.
6. **Forecast future crashes and victims**, based on historical trends, to anticipate future risks.

## Datasets
### 1. ICBC Reported Crashes
- **Source**: Insurance Corporation of British Columbia (ICBC), 
- **Coverage**: Crash data for Metro Vancouver from 2018 to 2022, including information on crash severity, vehicle types involved, crash locations, and victim counts.

### 2. British Columbia Population Data
- **Source**: Government of British Columbia
- **Coverage**: Population data for municipalities in British Columbia, specifically focusing on Metro Vancouver, for the period 2018 to 2022.

**Purpose**: To display the crash-to-population ratio from 2018 to 2022.

### 5. Time Series Analysis of Crashes
**Purpose**: To track the number of crashes and victims over time (2018-2022).  
**Explanation**: 
This chart shows the fluctuations in total crashes and victims on a month-by-month basis. Notable features include:
- A **significant drop** in crashes and victims from January to April 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic lockdown. The city’s shutdown led to fewer vehicles on the road, which reduced the number of accidents.
- By the end of 2022, a **historic peak** in total crashes occurred, reaching 16,236 accidents, though the number of victims remained relatively steady. This stability could suggest improvements in vehicle safety or emergency response.

**Additional Insights**:  
- The chart also highlights **Friday** as the day with the highest crash count (12,047 crashes), while **Sunday** has the lowest crash count (8,562 crashes). This indicates increased risk on Fridays, possibly due to higher traffic volumes as people commute.

## Conclusions
From the analysis of vehicle crashes in Metro Vancouver between 2018 and 2022, several key insights emerged:
- Crashes are most frequent in larger municipalities like **Vancouver, Surrey, and Burnaby**, where higher populations and traffic volumes contribute to more accidents.
- **West Vancouver** and **Richmond** have high crash rates per capita, necessitating targeted road safety interventions.
- **Surrey, Langley, and Delta** report the highest victim-to-crash ratios, indicating the need for improved safety measures to reduce crash severity.
- Specific streets like **152 St** and **Kingsway** require focused safety improvements to mitigate accident risks.

## Recommendations
1. **Implement safety measures** on streets with high crash frequencies, such as traffic calming devices and improved signage.
2. **Focus road safety interventions** on municipalities with high crash rates per capita, particularly West Vancouver, Richmond, and Vancouver.
3. **Enhance safety measures in Surrey, Langley, and Delta** to reduce crash severity and protect residents.
4. **Use the forecasted trends** to design proactive road safety campaigns and policies that anticipate future risks.
